Central Bank Digital Currencies: 87 Countries Now in Active Development

Henrik Johansson February 24, 2026

The landscape of central bank digital currencies has shifted from theoretical exploration to active implementation across the globe. As of early 2026, 87 countries representing more than 90% of global GDP have CBDC initiatives in pilot, development, or research phases. Eleven countries have fully launched digital currencies for retail use, while major economies including the European Union, United Kingdom, and United States have advanced significantly in technical testing and policy framework development. This acceleration reflects central banks' recognition that the future of money is digital—and their determination to shape rather than react to that future.

The motivations driving CBDC development vary significantly across economies. Emerging markets often prioritize financial inclusion, seeking to provide banking access to unbanked populations through mobile-first digital currencies that don't require traditional bank accounts. Advanced economies focus more on payment system efficiency, reducing settlement times and costs while maintaining central bank oversight of monetary transmission. Several nations cite currency sovereignty concerns, particularly the desire to provide domestic alternatives to dollar-denominated stablecoins and foreign payment platforms that could otherwise disintermediate local financial systems.

Design choices reveal fundamental differences in central bank philosophies about privacy, surveillance, and the role of commercial banks. China's digital yuan operates through a two-tier system where the central bank issues currency to commercial banks, which then distribute it to consumers—preserving traditional banking relationships while gaining transaction visibility. The European Central Bank's digital euro proposals emphasize privacy protections, with plans for offline-capable payments and limits on data retention. The Bahamas' Sand Dollar and Nigeria's eNaira have already demonstrated real-world retail CBDC operations, providing early evidence about adoption patterns and technical challenges.

Commercial banks face existential questions as CBDC architectures take shape. A central bank digital currency could theoretically disintermediate commercial banks entirely, allowing consumers to hold accounts directly with monetary authorities. Most proposals mitigate this risk through holding limits that prevent CBDCs from replacing significant bank deposits, maintaining the banking system's role in credit creation and financial intermediation. Nevertheless, banks must prepare for scenarios where their deposit franchises face competition from risk-free central bank money, potentially requiring higher deposit rates or enhanced services to retain customer funds.

Cross-border CBDC initiatives present both opportunities and complications. Project mBridge connects central banks in China, Thailand, Hong Kong, and the UAE in testing wholesale cross-border settlements that could reduce transaction costs and times from days to seconds. The Bank for International Settlements Innovation Hub has facilitated multiple experiments linking national systems. However, these projects raise complex questions about monetary sovereignty, capital flow controls, and regulatory coordination that remain unresolved. The geopolitical dimensions are unavoidable: CBDC networks could either reinforce or challenge the dollar's dominance in international payments depending on how standards evolve.

Privacy advocates have raised significant concerns about CBDC surveillance potential. Unlike physical cash, which offers transaction anonymity, digital currencies create persistent records that could enable unprecedented government monitoring of economic activity. Central banks have acknowledged these concerns with varying degrees of responsiveness. Technical solutions including zero-knowledge proofs and tiered privacy based on transaction size attempt to balance legitimate law enforcement needs against civil liberties. The political acceptability of CBDCs in democratic societies may ultimately depend on how persuasively these privacy safeguards are implemented and communicated.

For individual consumers and businesses, CBDC development creates both preparation opportunities and uncertainties. Companies processing significant payment volumes should monitor their jurisdictions' CBDC timelines to ensure systems compatibility when implementations launch. Investors in financial services should analyze which institutions face CBDC-related disruption and which might benefit from infrastructure provision or integration services. The broader population should engage with policy consultations that many central banks are conducting, as design decisions made now will shape monetary systems for decades. The transition to digital central bank money appears increasingly inevitable; the remaining questions concern timing, design, and how smoothly societies navigate the transformation.
