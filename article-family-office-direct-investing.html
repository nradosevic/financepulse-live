Family Offices Go Direct: The $6 Trillion Shift Bypassing Private Equity

Nathaniel Brooks February 22, 2026

Family offices managing over $6 trillion globally are increasingly cutting out the middleman. Rather than allocating to private equity funds and paying the industry's notorious "2 and 20" fee structure, ultra-wealthy families are building internal teams to source, evaluate, and execute direct investments. The shift represents one of the most significant changes in private market dynamics since institutional investors first embraced alternative assets, with implications for fund managers, entrepreneurs, and deal intermediaries alike.

The economics driving this trend are straightforward. A typical private equity fund charges 2% annual management fees on committed capital plus 20% of profits above a hurdle rate. Over a ten-year fund life, these fees can consume 30-40% of gross returns, dramatically reducing net performance. Family offices with sufficient scale can replicate the investment approach—or at least capture similar deal exposure—while retaining far more of the upside. As wealth concentration has created more families with investable assets exceeding $500 million, the number capable of supporting dedicated deal teams has grown substantially.

Operational complexity represents the primary barrier to direct investing. Private equity firms have spent decades building sourcing networks, due diligence capabilities, and operational improvement playbooks. Replicating this infrastructure requires significant investment in talent and systems. Many family offices have responded by specializing in sectors where the family has operating experience—a real estate developer's office focusing on property, a technology entrepreneur's office backing software companies. This focus leverages existing expertise while avoiding the breadth requirements of generalist private equity.

Co-investment alongside established private equity firms offers a middle path that has grown explosively. When PE funds make large investments, they often offer co-investment rights to limited partners—essentially allowing them to invest additional capital in specific deals without paying additional fees. Sophisticated family offices have professionalized their co-investment programs, moving from passive acceptance of offered deals to proactive relationship building with fund managers. The best co-investors now receive preferential access to the most attractive opportunities.

Entrepreneurs and company owners increasingly view family office capital as an attractive alternative to traditional private equity. Family offices can offer longer hold periods, since they lack the fund lifecycle constraints that force PE firms to exit investments within 5-7 years. They may be less aggressive about financial engineering and cost cutting. And they can provide patient capital for transitions—succession planning, minority growth investments, or "permanent capital" structures—that don't fit traditional buyout frameworks. This positioning creates deal flow opportunities that traditional funds cannot access.

The talent market has responded to shifting capital flows. Former private equity professionals increasingly find opportunities at family offices, attracted by lower pressure environments, equity participation in deals, and the ability to focus on investing rather than fundraising. Investment banks and advisors have built dedicated family office coverage teams. Conferences and networks connecting family offices to deal flow have proliferated. What was once an opaque corner of finance has developed its own infrastructure and professional community.

For entrepreneurs seeking capital, understanding the family office landscape has become essential. These investors bring different motivations, timelines, and governance expectations than traditional funds. Building relationships requires patience—family offices rarely compete in auction processes and prefer proprietary or lightly marketed transactions. But for the right opportunities, they can offer terms and partnership structures unavailable elsewhere. The $6 trillion in family office assets represents a deep pool of capital for those who learn to access it effectively.
