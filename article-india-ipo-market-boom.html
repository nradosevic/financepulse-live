India's IPO Boom: $48 Billion Raised as Domestic Investors Drive Markets

Anika Desai February 20, 2026

India has quietly become the world's busiest IPO market, with $48 billion raised in 2025 alone—surpassing the United States for the first time ever. The surge reflects a remarkable transformation in Indian capital markets, driven by explosive growth in domestic retail investor participation, maturing mutual fund infrastructure, and an economy producing companies at scale that global investors want to own. For international portfolio managers, India's primary market has shifted from peripheral consideration to essential allocation.

The retail investor revolution underpins India's IPO success. Demat accounts—the electronic securities holdings that Indian investors require—have grown from 40 million to over 150 million in just five years. Smartphone-based brokers have slashed trading costs and democratized market access. The systematic investment plan phenomenon has channeled billions monthly from household savings into equity mutual funds, creating a domestic institutional base that reduces dependence on foreign capital flows. IPOs routinely see retail subscriptions exceeding available shares by 50 or 100 times.

The composition of listings has evolved toward higher quality and greater scale. Early phases of India's equity market development saw many smaller, lower-quality companies accessing public markets. Recent years have brought IPOs from India's technology champions, leading financial services firms, and consumer companies with genuine competitive advantages. Life Insurance Corporation's 2022 listing—the largest in Indian history—demonstrated that the market could absorb institutional-sized offerings. Subsequent mega-deals have confirmed this capacity.

Valuations in India's primary market reflect both genuine growth potential and elements of froth. Indian equities trade at meaningful premiums to other emerging markets on conventional metrics, with IPO pricing often pushing those premiums further. Bulls argue that India's demographics, digitization, and structural reform trajectory justify elevated multiples. Skeptics note that similar arguments have supported overvalued markets before. Recent IPO performance has been mixed—strong first-day pops followed by varied subsequent returns—suggesting some valuation discipline emerging.

Regulatory evolution has improved market infrastructure and investor protection. SEBI, India's securities regulator, has tightened disclosure requirements, enhanced price discovery mechanisms, and cracked down on manipulation that marred earlier market eras. Corporate governance standards, while still variable, have improved substantially among larger companies. The introduction of direct listing and simplified compliance for smaller companies aims to broaden market access while maintaining quality standards.

For international investors, accessing India's IPO market requires navigating practical complexities. Foreign portfolio investor registration, though streamlined, still involves bureaucratic process. Settlement operates on a T+1 cycle that differs from most developed markets. Currency hedging costs can be substantial given rupee volatility. Many global investors access Indian IPOs through locally domiciled funds or ETFs rather than direct participation, accepting some tracking error in exchange for operational simplicity.

The pipeline of potential listings suggests continued momentum. Hundreds of venture-backed technology companies, family-owned industrial conglomerates, and financial services firms are evaluating public market exits. India's inclusion in major bond indices is bringing new foreign capital that may spill over into equities. While individual IPO outcomes will vary, the structural story of Indian capital market development appears durable. For investors willing to do the work, India's primary market offers exposure to one of the world's most dynamic growth stories.
