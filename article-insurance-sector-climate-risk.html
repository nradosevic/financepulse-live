The Insurance Industry's Climate Reckoning: $140 Billion in Annual Losses

Amanda Blackwell March 10, 2026

The property and casualty insurance industry is undergoing a fundamental reassessment of risk as climate-related losses shatter historical models. Global insured losses from natural catastrophes exceeded $140 billion in 2025, marking the fourth consecutive year above $100 billion and a level that actuarial models considered extreme tail risk just a decade ago. Major carriers are responding by withdrawing from vulnerable markets entirely, raising premiums by double digits annually, or both—creating ripple effects that threaten real estate values, mortgage availability, and economic stability in affected regions.

California and Florida have become ground zero for the insurance crisis, though the problems extend nationwide. In California, seven of the twelve largest homeowners insurers have either stopped writing new policies or significantly restricted coverage in wildfire-prone areas, affecting millions of homeowners. Florida faces similar dynamics from hurricane exposure, with premiums averaging over $4,000 annually—three times the national average—and climbing. State-backed insurers of last resort, designed as temporary backstops, have become primary carriers for hundreds of thousands of properties, creating concentrated taxpayer risk.

The actuarial profession is grappling with how to price risks that no longer follow historical patterns. Traditional models relied on decades of loss data to project future claims, but climate change has rendered backward-looking analysis increasingly unreliable. Wildfires burn hotter and faster, hurricanes intensify more rapidly, and "secondary perils" like convective storms and flooding cause losses at unprecedented frequency. Insurers are investing heavily in catastrophe modeling that incorporates climate projections, but uncertainty remains substantial. Some risks may simply be uninsurable at prices consumers can afford.

The withdrawal of private insurance creates a cascade of economic consequences. Mortgage lenders require insurance as a condition of financing, so uninsurable properties effectively become unsellable except to cash buyers willing to self-insure. Property values in affected areas have begun declining as this reality sets in. Local governments face shrinking tax bases while simultaneously needing to invest in resilience measures. The wealth effect of declining home values ripples through consumer spending and the broader economy, particularly in states where real estate represents a significant share of household assets.

Reinsurance markets—the insurers of insurers—are transmitting price signals globally that reflect this new risk environment. Swiss Re, Munich Re, and other major reinsurers have raised rates substantially and tightened terms, costs that primary insurers pass through to consumers. Capital has flowed toward insurance-linked securities and catastrophe bonds, but at yields that price in significantly elevated risk. The message from capital markets is clear: climate risk is real, it's here now, and it's expensive.

Policy responses vary widely by jurisdiction. Some states have attempted to freeze rates or mandate coverage, interventions that risk accelerating insurer withdrawal when claims outpace premiums. Others are investing in resilience measures—building codes, wildfire mitigation, flood infrastructure—that can reduce losses and eventually attract private capital back. Federal flood insurance reform remains stalled despite the National Flood Insurance Program's growing deficit. The absence of coordinated policy creates a patchwork of approaches that leaves many communities exposed.

For investors, the insurance industry transformation creates both risks and opportunities. Traditional property insurers face structural headwinds that pressure returns on equity and require careful underwriting to navigate. Conversely, companies providing climate analytics, resilience engineering, and parametric insurance products are seeing strong demand. Real estate investors must incorporate insurance availability and cost into due diligence, particularly for coastal and wildfire-adjacent properties. The $140 billion annual loss figure isn't an anomaly—it's the new baseline, and investment strategies must adapt accordingly.
