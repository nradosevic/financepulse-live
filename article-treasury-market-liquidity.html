Treasury Market Liquidity Crisis: Why the World's Deepest Market Is Struggling

Alexandra Petrov February 24, 2026

The U.S. Treasury market—$26 trillion in outstanding debt, the foundation of global finance, the benchmark for virtually every other interest rate—has a liquidity problem. Episodes of extreme volatility and price dislocations that would have been unthinkable a decade ago have become disturbingly regular. From the March 2020 panic to the October 2023 flash crash to several incidents since, the world's supposedly deepest and most liquid market has repeatedly shown fragility that threatens not just bond investors but the entire financial system.

The structural roots of the problem lie in a mismatch between market growth and dealer capacity. Outstanding Treasury debt has roughly tripled since 2010, yet primary dealer balance sheets—the banks that intermediate between buyers and sellers—have barely grown. Regulatory constraints implemented after the 2008 crisis, particularly leverage ratio requirements and supplementary leverage ratio rules, make it capital-intensive for banks to hold Treasury inventory. When selling pressure overwhelms available balance sheet, prices can gap violently even in what should be the world's safest and most liquid securities.

The rise of algorithmic and high-frequency trading has changed market dynamics in ways that amplify stress. During normal conditions, electronic market makers provide abundant liquidity at tight spreads. But these firms have limited capital and strong risk controls; when volatility spikes, they withdraw rapidly, leaving traditional dealers to absorb order flow they're ill-equipped to handle. The result is a "liquidity illusion"—ample depth in calm markets that vanishes precisely when it's needed most.

The Federal Reserve's changing role complicates the picture. During quantitative easing, the Fed absorbed massive Treasury supply, reducing the burden on private markets. As the central bank now reduces its holdings through quantitative tightening, private investors must absorb not only new issuance but also runoff from the Fed's portfolio. This additional supply hits a market already struggling with its existing load, contributing to the basis trade unwinding and other dislocations that have characterized recent years.

Foreign official buyers, once reliable absorbers of Treasury supply, have become less dependable. China has reduced holdings as geopolitical tensions increase. Japan's buyers face currency hedging costs that erode returns. Central banks diversifying reserves look to alternatives. The marginal buyer of Treasuries is increasingly a hedge fund or leveraged account—exactly the type of holder likely to sell during stress rather than provide stabilizing demand.

Proposed solutions range from technical fixes to structural reforms. Central clearing of Treasury trades, mandated by regulators and phasing in through 2026, should improve netting efficiency and reduce settlement risk. Standing repo facilities give dealers easier access to financing. Some advocate exempting Treasuries from leverage ratio calculations entirely, removing the regulatory disincentive to hold inventory. More radical proposals include having the Fed act as explicit market maker of last resort.

For investors, Treasury market fragility means rethinking assumptions about risk-free assets. Treasuries remain the safest credit in the world, but their price volatility has increased substantially. Holding to maturity eliminates mark-to-market risk but may not be feasible for leveraged portfolios or funds facing redemptions. Diversifying duration exposure across Treasury alternatives—agencies, high-quality corporates, international sovereigns—may reduce portfolio risk even if it introduces modest credit exposure. The era of assuming Treasury markets always function smoothly has definitively ended.
