Active ETFs Hit $500 Billion: The Death of Mutual Funds Accelerates

Douglas Kim February 15, 2026

Assets in actively managed ETFs have surpassed $500 billion, a milestone that seemed impossible just five years ago when the category held under $100 billion. The explosive growth represents a fundamental restructuring of the asset management industry, as the benefits that made ETFs dominant in passive investing—tax efficiency, intraday liquidity, transparency, lower costs—prove equally compelling for active strategies. Traditional mutual fund companies face an existential choice: convert or watch assets drain away to ETF-native competitors.

The tax advantages of the ETF structure drive much of the migration. Mutual funds must distribute capital gains to shareholders annually, creating tax liabilities regardless of whether individual investors sell shares. ETFs can minimize or eliminate these distributions through the creation-redemption mechanism, allowing gains to compound untaxed until the investor chooses to sell. For taxable accounts, this structural advantage can add 1-2% annually to after-tax returns—compounding to substantial differences over investment horizons measured in decades.

Regulatory changes enabled the active ETF boom. The SEC's 2019 ETF Rule streamlined the launch process and, crucially, allowed semi-transparent structures that protect proprietary portfolio strategies from immediate disclosure. Before this change, active managers resisted ETFs because daily holdings disclosure would allow competitors and front-runners to exploit their strategies. Semi-transparent or "non-transparent" active ETFs solved this problem, opening the floodgates for active managers to access the ETF wrapper.

Fixed income has emerged as the fastest-growing segment of active ETFs. Bond markets have always favored active management given their fragmented, dealer-intermediated structure. Leading bond fund managers have rapidly converted their strategies to ETF format, with several launches gathering billions in assets within their first year. The combination of active bond selection with ETF efficiency has proven particularly appealing to advisors building diversified portfolios for clients.

Traditional mutual fund complexes are responding with conversion strategies. Dimensional Fund Advisors converted its entire mutual fund lineup to ETFs in one of the largest such transitions. Others have launched "clone" ETFs that mirror existing mutual fund strategies, allowing investors to choose their preferred vehicle. The conversions create one-time tax events for mutual fund shareholders but position firms for continued asset gathering in the preferred format. Those who delay risk accelerating outflows.

Fee pressure has accompanied the structural shift. ETFs operate with lower expense ratios than comparable mutual funds—roughly 20-30% lower on average for similar strategies. This difference reflects both competitive dynamics and operational efficiencies: ETFs don't maintain transfer agent relationships with individual shareholders, reducing administrative costs. Active managers who once charged 1% or more find themselves pressured toward 50-60 basis points in ETF format. Margins compress, but asset growth can offset the revenue impact.

For individual investors, the active ETF revolution represents an unambiguous positive. Access to professional portfolio management has never been cheaper or more tax-efficient. The transparency of ETF holdings—even in semi-transparent structures where there's a modest disclosure lag—exceeds what mutual funds provide. The ability to trade intraday offers flexibility that some investors value, though it also creates temptation for counterproductive market timing. Those still holding actively managed mutual funds in taxable accounts should evaluate whether ETF alternatives offer superior after-tax returns for their investment objectives.
