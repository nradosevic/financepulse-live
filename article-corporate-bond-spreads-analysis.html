Corporate Bond Spreads at 15-Year Lows: Opportunity or Warning Sign?

Christine Mueller March 4, 2026

Investment-grade corporate bond spreads have compressed to just 85 basis points over Treasuries, levels not seen since the pre-crisis euphoria of 2007. High-yield spreads tell a similar story, with the option-adjusted spread on the Bloomberg High Yield Index touching 300 basis points—historically tight by any measure. For fixed-income investors, this compression raises a fundamental question: does tight credit reflect genuine improvement in corporate fundamentals, or has the market priced in a rosy scenario that leaves little margin for error?

The bull case for credit rests on legitimately strong corporate balance sheets. Investment-grade companies deleveraged aggressively following the pandemic, using the interval to extend maturities and lock in low rates. Interest coverage ratios remain healthy despite higher benchmark rates, as many issuers termed out their debt when financing was cheap. Default rates, while edging higher from historic lows, remain well below long-term averages. If the economy achieves the soft landing that consensus expects, credit losses should stay contained.

Technical factors have reinforced fundamental support. Insurance companies, pension funds, and other yield-seeking institutions face persistent demand for fixed-income assets, particularly those with attractive spreads over government bonds. Foreign buyers, especially from Japan and Europe where local yields remain lower, have added to demand for dollar-denominated corporate debt. New issuance, while robust, has been readily absorbed, with many deals oversubscribed by multiples of the offering size.

The bear case centers on asymmetric risk-reward at current spread levels. Credit spreads can only compress so much—zero is the floor—while they can widen dramatically during stress periods. The 2020 COVID shock saw investment-grade spreads blow out to over 370 basis points in a matter of weeks; the 2008 crisis pushed high-yield spreads above 2,000 basis points. Buying at historically tight levels means accepting most of the downside with limited upside potential. Total returns become almost entirely dependent on duration positioning rather than credit selection.

The maturity wall presents a specific concern for high-yield issuers. Approximately $350 billion in speculative-grade debt matures through 2027, much of it originally issued at rates far below current refinancing costs. Companies that extended at 5% yields may face 9-10% rates when they return to market, pressuring interest coverage and potentially tipping marginal credits into distress. The leveraged loan market, which finances many private equity-owned companies, faces similar dynamics with floating-rate debt already repricing higher.

Dispersion within credit markets offers selective opportunities even as index-level spreads appear unattractive. The gap between the tightest and widest spreads within both investment-grade and high-yield indices has widened, rewarding bottom-up credit selection. Sectors facing structural challenges—office real estate, certain retail segments, and companies with heavy near-term maturities—trade at meaningful discounts to their healthier peers. Active managers with strong research capabilities can potentially add value by avoiding the weakest credits and concentrating in resilient sectors.

For investors navigating this environment, duration positioning may matter more than credit selection at the index level. If recession arrives and rates fall, high-quality bonds will rally despite spread widening, while lower-quality credits face both rate and spread headwinds. Moving up in quality while maintaining duration exposure hedges against recession risk while still participating in any rate-driven rally. Those convinced of continued economic strength might extend credit risk selectively, but should acknowledge they're accepting an unfavorable risk-reward skew at current spread levels.
